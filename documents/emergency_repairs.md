# Emergency Repairs

## What Constitutes an Emergency Repair

Conditions that typically trigger immediate habitability obligations include: roof leaks causing interior water damage, non-functioning heating systems in cold climates, sewage backups, confirmed mold growth affecting air quality, structural defects that pose safety risks, and infestations of rodents, cockroaches, or bedbugs. Minor aesthetic issues, such as scuffed paint or worn carpet, generally do not rise to a habitability violation.

The implied warranty of habitability — a legal doctrine recognized in virtually every U.S. state — cannot be waived by the lease agreement. The landmark case Javins v. First National Realty Corp. (428 F.2d 1071) established that a landlord's failure to maintain habitable conditions can justify a tenant withholding rent.

## Landlord Response Time Requirements

An emergency repair, such as a burst pipe or a failed heating system in winter, typically requires immediate or next-day response under most state landlord-tenant codes. Non-emergency repairs generally require a reasonable time to complete, often interpreted as seven to fourteen days.

Landlords who fail to make necessary repairs within a reasonable period expose themselves to rent withholding, repair-and-deduct remedies, and potential damages claims.

## Tenant Remedies for Unaddressed Emergency Repairs

- **Termination of lease:** when the landlord's failure to maintain habitable conditions is severe and persistent, most states permit the tenant to terminate the lease without penalty.
- **Rent withholding:** in states that permit it, tenants may withhold rent until habitability conditions are remedied. The mechanics vary significantly by state — some require depositing withheld rent into escrow. Research your state's specific rules before withholding.
- **Repair and deduct:** many states allow tenants to arrange for necessary repairs themselves and deduct the cost from rent, up to a specified cap (often one month's rent). Documentation of the repair and the landlord's failure to act is essential.
- **Damages:** tenants may sue for compensation for costs incurred due to the landlord's breach, including alternative accommodation costs, relocation expenses, and consequential damages caused by uninhabitable conditions.
- **Constructive eviction claim:** if the landlord's conduct makes the property effectively uninhabitable, the tenant may be entitled to treat the lease as terminated and claim damages as if they were evicted.
