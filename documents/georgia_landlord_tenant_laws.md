# Georgia Landlord-Tenant Handbook

> **Introduction**
>
> This Handbook provides an overview and answers common questions about Georgia residential landlord-tenant law. The information in this Handbook does not apply to commercial or business leases.
>
> The best solution for each case depends on the facts. Because facts in each case are different, this Handbook covers general terms and answers, and those answers may not apply to your specific problem. While this publication may be helpful to both landlords and tenants, it is not a substitute for professional legal advice. This Handbook has information on Georgia landlord-tenant law as of the last revision date and may not be up to date on the law. Before relying on this Handbook, independently research and analyze the relevant law based on your specific problem, location, and facts.
>
> In Georgia, there is not a government agency that can intervene in a landlord-tenant dispute or force the landlord or tenant to behave a particular way. Landlords or tenants who cannot resolve a dispute need to use the courts, either themselves or through a lawyer, to enforce their legal rights.
>
> The Handbook is available on the internet from the Georgia Department of Community Affairs: https://dca.georgia.gov/

## Who is a Tenant?

Determining whether you are entering into a landlord-tenant relationship is important because tenants have certain rights that non-tenants (like guests or squatters) do not have. Even if a landlord does not charge rent, a landlord-tenant relationship might be created if the landlord gave you the right to stay at the property. Start by reading your written agreement, if one exists, and look for language referring to you as a "tenant" or "lessee" or "resident." An agreement should give the tenant the "right to possess" the property, but other terms might also make it clear that you are a tenant. If the landlord did not set out a specific end date, there could be a tenancy-at-will.

**You are more likely to be considered a tenant if your agreement requires that you:**
- Take responsibility for the security of people and personal items inside
- Provide your own furniture
- Take out the trash
- Keep the premises clean

**You are less likely to be considered a tenant if your agreement prohibits you from:**
- Making any alterations, such as hanging pictures
- Performing maintenance
- Having visitors
- Coming and going at certain hours

**If you do not have a written agreement**, an oral agreement or the conduct of the parties will determine if you are a tenant. One or more of the following factors would make it more likely you are a tenant:
- You have been paying rent
- You use the property as your primary residence
- You have personal belongings at the property
- You have been staying at the property for several months or more
- You have no end date for when you have to leave the property
- You clean and maintain the property
- You can come and go from the property without interference from others

> **Who is a squatter?** Under Georgia law, a squatter is a person who never had the property owner's permission to come on the property. A property owner can swear an affidavit to have the person removed without an eviction process and the person squatting may face criminal penalties.

Hotel/motel guests are typically not tenants, but an extended stay guest could become a tenant based on an express agreement or conduct of the parties.

## Discrimination

### Discrimination Prohibited

Landlords[^1] cannot discriminate based on a person's "protected class." The Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) is the main federal agency that tenants can turn to if a landlord has discriminated against them.

[^1]: The Federal and Georgia Fair Housing Acts cover most housing, however in some circumstances the law exempts owner-occupied buildings with no more than four units, single-family housing sold or rented without the use of a broker, and housing operated by organizations and private clubs that limit occupancy to members.

### Protected Classes

Protected classes include race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status[^2], or disability. Discrimination can take many forms, including:

- **Refusal to rent:** refusing to rent to a person because they are a member of a protected class
- **Discouragement:** engaging in conduct that discourages a person from renting or makes housing unavailable to a person because they are a member of a protected class (including failing to tell the person of marketing promotions, rent reductions, privileges, or services associated with the property because of their protected class)
- **Different treatment:** imposing different terms and conditions on members of a protected class[^3]
- **Separation:** steering members of a protected class to particular buildings or units away from other units
- **Exclusion:** not advertising to members of a protected class
- **Concealment:** falsely telling a member of a protected class that a unit is not available
- **Preference:** making any statement that indicates a preference based on a protected class

[^2]: Families are protected when one or more children under the age of 18 live(s) with (1) a parent; (2) a person who has legal custody; or (3) a designee of the parent or legal custodian, with the parent or legal custodian's written permission. "Family" also includes pregnant women and anyone in the process of securing legal custody of a child under the age of 18.

[^3]: Refusing to rent to applicants with a criminal record may be discrimination if the refusal has an unjustified discriminatory effect on a protected class, such as Hispanics and African Americans, who have higher than average incarceration rates. To avoid discrimination, a landlord should evaluate each applicant's history on a case-by-case basis, taking into account the nature, severity, and age of a conviction.

Discrimination can also be indirect, such as an apartment rule that appears neutral but is applied in a way that causes a protected group to suffer. If the business owner does not have a legitimate business justification for the rule, it is discrimination. In addition, it is illegal for anyone to threaten, coerce, intimidate, or interfere with anyone exercising a fair housing right or assisting others who exercise that right.

More information on housing discrimination can be found on HUD's website: https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp

### Rights of Disabled Tenants

The Fair Housing Act also protects individuals with disabilities. Landlords must make reasonable accommodations when necessary to allow equal access, permit reasonable changes, and satisfy certain accessibility requirements.

> **Requesting accommodations:** If a tenant or applicant has a disability that requires accommodations, someone must request that the landlord make the necessary accommodations. The person with the disability, a family member, or someone else acting on the person's behalf, can request the accommodation.

**Reasonable accommodations.** Landlords must change rules, policies, practices, or services when a reasonable accommodation is necessary for a disabled person to use and enjoy a housing program or rental unit. Reasonable accommodations may be necessary at all housing stages, including when applying to rent, living in the unit, or preventing eviction.

To be defined as a "handicapped person," the person must either (1) have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, (2) have a history of such an impairment, or (3) be viewed as having such an impairment.

Landlords should do everything they can to assist, but they are not required to make changes that would fundamentally alter the housing program or create an undue financial and administrative burden.

*Examples of reasonable accommodations:* Waiving a no-pet policy for a tenant who needs an assistive animal or providing an assigned parking place close to accessible apartments for a tenant with a disability that affects their ability to walk.

**Reasonable changes.** A landlord must allow a disabled tenant to make, at the tenant's expense, reasonable changes to their unit that are necessary to allow the disabled person full use of the premises. A tenant may be required to restore the premises to their original condition upon leaving the unit, if reasonable. The landlord must also permit reasonable changes to common areas to make them usable. In most cases, it would be unreasonable for the landlord to require the tenant to return the common areas to their original condition.

**Accessibility requirements.** Newly constructed multifamily dwellings with four or more units must provide basic accessibility to persons with disabilities if the buildings were first ready for occupancy after March 13, 1991, including:
- One entrance to the building on a route so that people with wheelchairs can access it
- Accessibility to public areas such as a lobby or swimming pool
- Doors wide enough to accommodate people in wheelchairs
- Accessibility to each unit (if there is no elevator, only all ground floor units must be accessible)
- Enough reinforcement in bathroom walls to allow a tenant to install grab bars where needed
- Light switches and other controls located low enough for use by a person in a wheelchair
- Kitchens and bathrooms designed so that a wheelchair user can maneuver within the space

### If You Believe You Have Been the Victim of Discrimination

- To determine whether you have been discriminated against, you may contact HUD's Fair Housing Initiatives Program ("FHIP"). Organizations that participate in HUD's FHIP may be able to speak to a housing provider on your behalf, conduct an investigation to help determine if you experienced discrimination, or otherwise provide you with information and assistance.

  Georgia has two FHIP organizations: one in Hinesville (912-877-4243) and another in Atlanta (404-524-0000). More information: https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp/contact_fhip

- If you want to file a complaint with HUD's Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity (FHEO) branch, the complaint must be filed within **one (1) year** of the discriminatory act. File online at: https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp/online-complaint

- You can speak with an FHEO intake specialist by calling **1-800-669-9777** or **1-800-877-8339** (TTY). The FHEO's regional office for Georgia is in Atlanta: **404-331-5140** or **800-440-8091**.

- FHEO will investigate your complaint and, if appropriate, try to reach an agreement with the landlord. If an agreement cannot be reached and FHEO has reason to believe you were discriminated against, FHEO will allow you to choose between (a) having an administrative hearing with an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) or (b) sending the case to federal court. An ALJ can order the landlord to:
  - Compensate you for actual damages
  - Provide injunctive or other equitable relief
  - Pay the federal government a civil penalty
  - Pay reasonable attorney's fees and costs

- As long as you have not signed an agreement and the ALJ has not started a hearing, you can file a lawsuit in federal or state court within **two (2) years** of the discriminatory action.

## Entering Into a Lease

A lease grants a tenant the right to use and live in the rental property temporarily, so the landlord can have the property back in the future.

> **Lease terms are important.** A lease is a **contract** that defines the rights and responsibilities that the landlord and tenant owe each other. Once you sign the lease, you cannot change your mind later. If the tenant changes their mind and decides not to move into the unit after signing the lease, the landlord can impose early termination penalties if provided in the lease. **Pay close attention to the details!**

### Submitting a Rental Application

The first step most landlords require is the rental application.

- **Application Fees.** Application fees may be required and are usually not refundable, even if the application is denied or you change your mind. The fee may be applied to the first month's rent. Always get a receipt for any fee or deposit.

- **Information on Application.** Landlords commonly request: legal name, social security number, employer's name, job title and annual income, past employment information, references, identity of nearest relative, and consent for a credit report and criminal record check.

- **Background Check.** Landlords may require you to agree to a credit and criminal background check as part of the application. Credit reporting agencies can provide information about you to a potential landlord without your consent.

### Reviewing and Signing a Lease

If the landlord accepts your application, the next step is to enter into a rental agreement called a lease.

- **Landlords should be careful** about language included or left out of a lease and consider consulting with an attorney who regularly handles landlord/tenant legal issues.

- **Tenants should *always* read the lease before signing.** Leases and rental agreements differ from landlord to landlord. As the tenant, you must follow the terms in a lease even if you did not understand or read it. You may request a copy of the lease to review a day or two in advance. You should always request a copy of the signed lease and save it in a safe place.

#### It is a violation of the law if your lease states:

- The landlord removes or reduces their responsibility to maintain habitable property in good repair
- The landlord removes or reduces their responsibility to respond to damages caused by their failure to keep the property in good repair
- The lease says the landlord does not have to follow local laws
- The lease says the landlord does not have to follow Georgia security deposit law
- The lease says the landlord can evict you without going through the eviction process in court
- The lease requires payment of the landlord's attorney fees if the tenant breaches the lease but does not require the landlord to pay tenant's attorney fees if the landlord breaches the lease

#### Terms you may want to ask about:
- Very long lease terms with early termination penalties or fees
- Automatic rent increases during the lease term
- References to rules that you have not received
- How utilities will be paid
- When and how the landlord can access the unit

#### Key Lease Considerations

**Written vs. Oral Lease.** A written lease provides clarity and helps resolve disputes. Oral leases often lead to misunderstandings and can be terminated with less notice.

**Lease Length.** Leases can be made for any length of time. NOTE: Additional requirements may apply to leases longer than a year.

> **Expecting a job transfer? Moving soon?** If you are about to change jobs or want to purchase a home, you can ask for the right to terminate your lease. But without special written termination provisions, the tenant cannot terminate the lease early without penalty.

**Renter's Insurance.** A landlord's property insurance typically does not cover a tenant's personal items damaged due to fire, theft, or water, so purchasing renter's insurance is often advisable.

**Occupancy Limits.** Georgia does not regulate the number of people who can live in rental housing, but local ordinances may. Generally, restricting two (2) people per bedroom is reasonable.

**Utilities.** Ask whether you or the landlord will pay utilities. Some landlords may employ "master metering," which means multiple tenants' usage is measured on the same meter and costs are divided among tenants.

NOTE: If you are facing eviction, it is illegal for a landlord to knowingly and willfully suspend your utilities (heat, cooling, light, and water service) until after the judge's final decision.

**Lease with an Option to Purchase.** You may consider a lease with an option to purchase if you want to buy a home. Additional money may be paid as a lump sum at the start or in the form of higher monthly rent.

**Flooding.** If a residential rental property has flooded at least three (3) times in the past five (5) years and damaged the living space, the landlord must notify the potential tenant in writing before entering a lease. If the landlord does not give notice, the landlord is liable for property damage from flooding during the lease.

**Security Deposit When Moving In.** The security deposit protects the landlord if a tenant moves out owing money or having damaged the property. If you give proper notice and vacate without owing rent or causing damage, the landlord must return the security deposit within **thirty (30) days**.

Security deposit rules include:
- **Deposit limit:** Cannot exceed two (2) months' rent
- **Escrow Account:** Landlords who own more than ten (10) rental units or contract with a management agent must place the deposit in a bank escrow account and give tenants written notice of its location
- **Transfer of Deposit:** If the property is sold, the former owner must either transfer the deposit to the new owner or refund it to the tenant
- **Refundable Deposit:** Application fees or deposits to hold an apartment are not considered security deposits and usually are not refundable. Pet deposits may be refundable depending on the lease terms

> **Using AI:** Potential tenants can copy and paste their unsigned lease into free artificial intelligence programs, like ChatGPT, and ask to highlight important or potentially unfavorable lease terms. This should NOT substitute for reading the entire lease or consulting an attorney if necessary.

**Move-in Inspection.** A formal move-in inspection process is required for landlords who own more than ten (10) rental units or employ a management agent. Both you and the landlord must sign the damage list, and the tenant should keep a copy.

> **CAUTION!** The landlord will *not* be responsible to repair problems with your unit that were obvious during the move-in walk-through unless those problems make the unit unsafe or uninhabitable. Inspect your rental thoroughly and request needed repairs during move-in.

**Promised repairs.** Get the landlord to agree to any promised repairs **in writing**.

**Name and address of the property owner or authorized agency.** You must receive this information at the time the lease is signed. If any changes occur during the lease term, the landlord should give you notice within 30 days.

**House Rules.** Ask for a copy of any house rules before signing the lease. A lease may allow the landlord to terminate the lease or evict a tenant for violating house rules.

#### What Should Be in a Lease?

- Names of the tenant, the landlord or the landlord's agent, and the property manager
- Description of the unit, including appliances and heating/cooling sources
- Description of the real property (if a single-family home)
- How long the lease is and when it ends
- Amount of rent and payment due date, including any grace period, late charges, or returned check charges
- How rent will be delivered and acceptable payment methods
- How to terminate the lease early, including any early termination fees
- Security deposit amount
- How and when utilities will be paid
- Amenities and common facilities available for the tenant's use
- House Rules, including pets, guests, noise, and consequences for rule violations
- Parking available for the tenant's use
- Pest control schedule, if provided
- Handling of tenant repair requests, including emergency requests
- Circumstances allowing the landlord to enter the rental unit, including notice requirements

## Issues During a Lease

### Repairs and Maintenance

Residential landlords have a duty to keep a unit in good repair and in a safe and habitable condition.

**The landlord must:**
- Maintain the building structure
- Keep electric, heating, cooling, and plumbing in working order
- Exercise ordinary care to keep the unit safe for tenants

**Unless the lease says something else, the landlord is NOT responsible for:**
- Problems that were obvious during the move-in inspection, unless they make the unit unsafe or uninhabitable
- Carpet cleaning
- Providing appliances or fences (EXCEPT if the landlord already provided these, then the landlord IS responsible for repairing them)

> **Notice of repair required!** You must give prompt notice to the landlord of any problem(s) needing repair and **written notice is best**. Notice must follow the requirements in the lease.

> **CAUTION!** Even if the landlord fails to make repairs, you generally **must continue to pay rent**. If you do not pay rent when it is due, the landlord can begin the eviction process.

#### What if a landlord fails to repair within a reasonable time after receiving notice?

- **File a lawsuit.** A judge may make the landlord pay for damages caused by the failure to repair if the landlord had notice and opportunity to repair but failed to do so.

- **Repair-and-deduct.** Tenants can have a qualified and licensed professional make the required repair at a reasonable cost and subtract the cost from future rent payments. Tenants should:
  - Notify the landlord in writing **before** arranging for the repair
  - Get estimates from multiple vendors and keep copies
  - Ideally get the landlord to agree to the cost before beginning the repair
  - Keep copies of repair receipts and ask the professional for a statement detailing the work
  - Subtract the repair costs from the next rent payment

  CAUTION! Landlords can argue the repair was unnecessary or completed at an unreasonable cost. You cannot use repair-and-deduct to repair common areas.

- **Contact the local, county, or city housing code inspector.** Landlords must comply with local housing codes. If the property is condemned and residential use prohibited, you can treat the landlord as having broken the lease. It is ILLEGAL for a landlord to evict you or retaliate against you for requesting a repair or calling code enforcement.

- **Move out.** If the landlord's failure to repair makes the unit unfit to live in, this may be considered "constructive eviction." Constructive eviction requires that:
  1. The landlord's failure has caused the unit to become unfit to live in
  2. The unit cannot be restored to a fit condition by ordinary repairs
  3. You move out of the premises

  CAUTION! If you abandon the unit without a defense of constructive eviction, the landlord may end the lease, find another tenant while still holding you responsible for remaining rent, or leave the unit empty and continue to collect rent from you.

> **Can a tenant change the locks?** Typically, the lease will say that the tenant cannot change locks without the landlord's permission. If the lease is silent, you may technically change locks but must give the landlord new keys when you move out.

#### Landlord is Not Paying Utility Bills

If the landlord pays utilities and the service is from a Public Service Commission-regulated provider (e.g., Georgia Power): the power company must give tenants at least **five (5) days** written notice prior to turning off utilities. The electricity provider must accept payments from you for your portion of any past-due amounts.

NOTE: During an eviction case, it is illegal for a landlord to knowingly and willfully turn off utilities until after the judge makes a final decision.

#### Visitors' and Tenants' Use of the Rental Unit

- **Visitors.** Unless the lease says you cannot, you may have visitors as long as they do not disrupt other residents. Do not allow visitors to appear to be living at the unit without the landlord's permission.
- **Pets.** A violation of pet rules may allow the landlord to terminate the lease and pursue eviction.
- **Noisy Neighbors.** Contact the landlord first. Police should only be contacted as a last resort.
- **Altering the Unit.** Generally, you cannot substantially or permanently alter leased property without the landlord's permission.

#### Landlord Access to the Rental Unit

The lease will establish under what conditions a landlord may enter. Typically, landlords can have reasonable access after giving notice. If the lease does not give the landlord the right to enter, you can legally refuse access except in emergencies.

#### Changes to Lease Terms

- Rent can only be increased during a lease if the lease says that may happen
- If an apartment complex changes owners, the new owners are generally subject to existing leases
- If you are a tenant-at-will, the landlord must give **60 days' notice** of any rent increases

### Subletting

A person who leases a unit from the original tenant is called a "subtenant." The lease determines whether the original tenant can sublet. Often, subletting requires the landlord's permission. The original tenant remains responsible to the landlord for rent and any damage caused by the subtenant.

### Ending the Lease

#### Early Termination

The landlord and tenant may only end a written lease according to its terms, with limited exceptions for military service members or tenants fleeing family violence.

- **Early Termination Fees.** May be required under the lease. Fees are allowed if (1) the landlord's damages from early termination are difficult to estimate accurately, (2) the fees are intended to cover damages rather than penalize the tenant, and (3) the fees are a reasonable estimate of damages.

- **Duty to Continue Paying Rent.** In Georgia, if the tenant abandons the unit without permission or legal exception, the landlord does not have to find another tenant and can hold the tenant responsible for rent through the end of the lease.

- **Family Violence and Stalking Exception.** A tenant can terminate their lease with **thirty (30) days notice** if they have a court order for protection. See page 23 for more information.

- **Military Service Members.** May have more flexibility to relocate and terminate early based on military orders. See page 22 for more information.

> **Ending a Tenancy-at-will:** The landlord must give the tenant **sixty (60) days' notice** to leave, or sixty (60) days' notice before changing the rent. A tenant can end a tenancy-at-will by giving the landlord **thirty (30) days' notice**.

#### End of the Lease Term

- **Renewal.** A lease may allow renewal by signing a new lease. If you do not timely renew, the landlord may treat the lease as expired.
- **Extension.** A lease may allow automatic extension at the end of the current lease without signing a new lease.
- **Termination.** If the lease expired without renewal or extension, the landlord can take back the property. If the landlord continues to accept monthly rent after expiration, a tenancy-at-will is created.

### Move-Out and Security Deposit

#### Move-out Inspection

Landlords who own more than ten (10) rental units or employ a management agent must provide a formal inspection at move-out or return the security deposit.

- Within **three (3) business days** after the lease terminates, the landlord must inspect and prepare a list of all damage with estimated dollar values.
- You may inspect the unit within **five (5) business days** after termination.
- If you agree with the damage listed, you cannot later contest the landlord keeping the deposit.

#### Deductions from the Security Deposit

The landlord **cannot** keep the security deposit to cover normal wear and tear. The landlord **may** keep part of the deposit for:
- Damage caused by you, household members, pets, or guests (intentional, accidental, or careless)
- Unpaid rent or late charges
- Unpaid pet fees
- Unpaid utilities that were the tenant's responsibility
- Damage caused by early termination

The repair/replacement amount should be based on the condition when the tenant moved in (e.g., depreciated value of a ten-year-old carpet, not a new carpet).

#### Returning the Security Deposit

All landlords must return the security deposit within **thirty (30) days** after termination of the lease or the date tenants leave, whichever is last. If held because of damage, the landlord must send notice identifying the damage, estimated dollar amounts, and any refund.

#### Wrongfully Withheld Security Deposit

You may sue to recover the deposit in the county where the landlord resides. You may also sue for interest, attorney fees, and filing costs. A landlord who owns more than ten units or uses a management agent can be liable for **three (3) times the amount withheld** plus attorney fees, unless the withholding was a mistake despite reasonable efforts to avoid it.

### Landlord Retaliation

In Georgia, landlords cannot retaliate against tenants who request repairs or take other actions related to life, health, safety, or habitability.[^9]

[^9]: O.C.G.A. § 44-7-24.

**Protected tenant actions:**
- Asking the landlord to repair the property
- Establishing, attempting to establish, or participating in a tenant organization
- Filing a good faith complaint with a government entity about housing or building code violations
- Attempting to enforce a right or remedy under the lease or permitted by law

**Landlord retaliation is presumed** if the landlord takes any of the following actions within **three (3) months** of the tenant's protected action:
- Filing a dispossessory (eviction) action
- Depriving the tenant access to the property
- Reducing services to the tenant
- Increasing the rent
- Terminating the written or oral lease
- Interfering materially with the tenant's rights under the lease

**Landlord's permitted actions** (not considered retaliatory, even if within 3 months):
- Increasing rent or reducing services under terms permitted in a written lease
- Increasing rent or reducing services as part of a pattern for the residential complex or under a state/federal housing program
- Filing dispossessory or terminating the lease because:
  - The tenant is behind on rent
  - The tenant, family member, or guest intentionally damages the property or threatens safety
  - The tenant breaches the lease by committing serious misconduct or criminal acts
  - The tenant holds over after giving notice of termination or after the landlord gives notice at the end of a written lease term

**Retaliation remedies.** If a retaliation claim is successful, the tenant may recover one (1) month's rent plus **$500.00**, court costs, and possible attorney fees, minus any balance owed to the landlord.

## Evictions

In Georgia, landlords cannot kick out a tenant or prevent a tenant's access to a unit without first going through the court dispossessory process, commonly known as eviction. Self-help evictions by the landlord are illegal, even if you have violated the lease. *During the eviction process, you are allowed to remain in the property with active utilities until there is a court decision.*

### Reasons for Eviction

A tenant can be evicted for not paying rent, for failing to move out at the expiration or termination of the lease, or for violating the lease.

If the landlord tries to evict a tenant for not paying rent, the tenant has **seven (7) days** once dispossessory is served to "tender" or pay the rent and any fees owed, plus court costs. This is a complete defense, but the tenant may only use the tender defense with the landlord **once in a 12-month period**.

### Eviction Notice

**Read the lease.** The landlord must comply with lease terms regarding notice and termination.

**Demand for possession.** The landlord must demand that the tenant give up possession and leave. This demand does not need to be in writing (unless evicting for unpaid rent), but the landlord should keep a record of how and when it was made.

A demand should be dated, list the tenant's name and premises, and may state: *"You [tenant] are notified that you have violated or failed to perform terms of the Lease as follows: \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_. You must surrender possession and vacate the premises within \_\_\_\_ days after service of this notice."*

**Notice of failure to pay rent.** At least **three (3) business days** before a landlord files dispossessory for unpaid rent, the landlord must give the tenant written notice to pay the back rent and fees. The notice can be served by any method in the lease or posted in a sealed envelope on the door of the unit.

### Eviction Process

#### 1. File a Dispossessory Affidavit

If the tenant refuses or fails to leave, the landlord files a dispossessory affidavit under oath in magistrate court in the county where the property is located. The affidavit must state: (1) landlord's name, (2) tenant's name, (3) reason for removal, (4) verification that the landlord demanded possession and was refused, and (5) the amount of rent or money owed (if any).

NOTE: After filing a dispossessory action based on nonpayment, the landlord will generally not accept rent (to avoid giving you a defense). However, the landlord can request the court order you to pay rent to the court if the process takes longer than two (2) weeks.

NOTE: In Georgia, the landlord can request a distress warrant to seize the tenant's property to secure payment of rent when the tenant seeks to remove their property from the premises.

#### 2. Service of the Dispossessory Affidavit

In most counties, the sheriff will serve you. There are three ways a summons can be served:
1. Personally delivered
2. Delivered to a competent adult who resides in the unit
3. **(Most Common)** "Tack and mail" — tacked on the door and sent by first class mail. The date stamped on the envelope should match the date it was tacked. Tack and mail service is only acceptable if no one was home when the sheriff tried personal service. If served by tack and mail and the tenant did not file an answer or appear in court, the court can still order the tenant to move, but **cannot award rent or other money damages**.

#### 3. Tenant Answer

The summons requires a response either orally or in writing within **seven (7) days** from the date of service. If the tenant does not answer, the court can evict and send the sheriff to remove the tenant.

> **Answering the summons is necessary to claim any defenses.** The answer is the tenant's chance to explain why the landlord is not legally entitled to evict.

- **What to include in the answer:** Any defenses against eviction and any counterclaims against the landlord. If you don't include defenses or counterclaims, you may not be able to bring them up later.
- **The tenant can also file in the answer:** Foreseeable damages caused by a wrongfully-filed eviction, such as time spent filing an answer, work hours missed, travel expenses, and attorney fees.
- **Answer with truthful statements:** Purposefully making a false statement in a court filing can be a crime.

NOTE: If the case cannot be decided within **two (2) weeks** of when the tenant was served, the tenant must pay the court past rent owed and future rent as it becomes due. Failure to pay will result in eviction.

> **Tender Defense:** Tenants may be able to avoid eviction if they pay all rent and fees alleged plus court costs within **seven (7) days** of receiving the dispossessory. The tenant may only use this defense **once in a twelve (12) month period**.

#### 4. Hearing

If the tenant files an answer stating a valid defense, a hearing will be held, usually within **seven (7) days**. If the tenant does not raise a valid legal defense, the court may dismiss the answer and evict the tenant without a hearing.

> **No court hearing?** The tenant may not get a hearing if they did not file an answer and assert valid defenses. Assert all valid defenses in your answer — do not wait to raise issues at the hearing.

#### 5. Writ of Possession

If the court rules in the landlord's favor, the landlord requests a writ of possession requiring the tenant to move after **seven (7) days** and pay past rent owed. Once a writ is issued:
- The sheriff will supervise the landlord's removal of a tenant who refuses to leave. The landlord is responsible for eviction costs.
- When a tenant's personal property is removed, it must be placed on some portion of the landlord's land.
- Once judgment has been entered in the landlord's favor, the tenant can still be removed even if they pay the landlord.
- When a landlord evicts a tenant and takes possession of the property, the lease is terminated and the tenant does not owe future rent.

#### Appeals

- A **landlord** can appeal within **seven (7) days** from the date judgment was entered. A request for jury trial must be within thirty (30) days from the filing of the appeal.
- A **tenant** can appeal within **seven (7) days** from the date judgment was entered. To file an appeal, the tenant must pay court costs or file a "pauper's affidavit." An appeal prevents a writ of possession from being executed, but the tenant must pay all rent and fees due to continue living in the unit while the appeal is pending.

## Miscellaneous

### Military Service Members as Tenants

Active members of the military have protection under state and federal law. The Service Members Civil Relief Act ("SCRA"):

**Affords relief if military service makes rent payment difficult.** Before a court can evict, it must find that the service member's ability to pay rent was not materially affected by their military service. If the member's ability to pay was materially affected, the court may postpone the eviction for up to **ninety (90) days**.

This rule applies when: (1) The landlord is attempting eviction when the service member is in military service or after being ordered to report to duty; (2) The rented premises is used for housing by the spouse, children, or other dependents of the service member; and (3) The agreed rent does not exceed a certain maximum (as of 2023: $9,106.46 per month, subject to change).

**The SCRA provides an option to postpone court proceedings.** If service members are unable to appear in court due to military duties, they may postpone the proceeding for a mandatory minimum of 90 days. The written request must (1) explain why current military duty materially affects the service member's ability to appear; (2) provide a date when the service member can appear; and (3) include a letter from the commander. If the court denies additional delays, an attorney must be appointed to represent the service member.

**The SCRA allows default judgments to be set aside.** If a default judgment is entered during active-duty service, or within 60 days thereafter, the service member may reopen and set it aside if they can show they were not harmed by not appearing and that they have good legal defenses.

**The SCRA permits relocation and early lease termination.** Service members may terminate leases without penalty by delivering written notice and a copy of their military orders. For monthly leases, the lease is terminated effective 30 days after the first date on which the next rental payment is due after giving notice.

Georgia law also allows a service member who meets certain requirements to terminate a lease entered into on or after July 1, 2005 by giving the landlord a **30 day advance written notice** of termination.

More information: https://www.military.com/benefits/military-legal-matters/scra/scra-rental-and-eviction-protection.html

### Family Violence and Stalking Protections

Both Georgia law and the federal Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) provide protections for tenants who are victims of family violence or stalking (including women, men, and children).

**Early lease termination.** Georgia law allows tenants to terminate their leases early when a civil or criminal family violence order or civil or criminal stalking order has been issued to protect the tenant or their minor children.[^13] The tenant must provide the landlord with a copy of the order and give **thirty (30) days notice** before the lease is terminated.

[^13]: O.C.G.A. § 44-7-23.

**Additional Protections for Tenants.** VAWA provides protections for victims of domestic violence, dating violence, sexual assault, or stalking. Protections are available to all individuals regardless of sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation. These protections can include the right to report a crime, an emergency transfer to another housing unit, or lease bifurcation to remove the abusive household member.

For help and advice on escaping an abusive relationship, call the **National Domestic Violence Hotline** at **1-800-799-SAFE (7233)** or **1-800-787-3224 (TTY)**.

More information from HUD: https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp/VAWA
More information from DCA: https://dca.georgia.gov/housing-choice-voucher/participants

### Roommates

- Each roommate in a tenant relationship with the landlord can be held responsible for the **full amount of the rent due**, unless the lease describes otherwise ("joint and several" liability). If you end up paying more than your share, you can sue your former roommate to recover the difference.
- Landlords collect money from tenants who actually signed the lease. Roommates who did not sign the lease are not liable to the landlord unless a landlord-tenant relationship was established.
- Security deposits are usually divided equally among the tenants.
- Landlords do not have legal means to step in and resolve roommate disputes unless there is a lease violation or criminal act.

### Lead Based Paint Disclosures

Landlords who rent units built **before 1978** must disclose the presence of all known lead-based paint and lead hazards in the rental unit and common areas, unless the property is determined to be free of lead-based paint by a state-certified inspector. Lofts, studios, or short-term leases of less than 100 days are exempt.

**Landlords must:**
- Give tenants an EPA-approved pamphlet entitled "Protect Your Family from Lead in Your Home"
- Disclose any known information concerning lead-based paint or lead hazards, including location and condition
- Provide any available records and reports on lead-based paint or lead hazards
- Include a Lead Warning Statement in the lease or as an attachment; landlords, agents, and tenants must sign and date it
- If a child under six (6) is found to have lead poisoning and resides in a dwelling with lead hazards, the owner must take steps to reduce the lead hazards

If you are a tenant who did not receive the disclosure and you have been damaged by lead paint, call **1-800-424-LEAD (5323)** or contact a lawyer. You may be able to recover triple the amount of your actual damages.

### Foreclosure

If a property is foreclosed, the original landlord-tenant relationship ends and you may be subject to eviction. You are not automatically evicted when someone new gains ownership. The new owner may instead decide to establish a new landlord-tenant relationship with you. If the new owners start eviction proceedings, accepting or demanding rent from you can create a valid tenancy-at-will.

### Manufactured and Mobile Homes

Mobile homes are considered personal property (not "real property") unless they are permanently affixed to the land. The rules in this handbook may not apply to mobile homes in the same ways.

**Repairs to Mobile Homes:**
- Tenants who rent a mobile home from someone who does not own the land cannot use landlord-tenant law to demand repairs unless the lease says so
- The landowner is not responsible for repairing the mobile home unless the landowner owns the mobile home and it is built into the land. However, the landowner must keep the lot and common areas in good repair.

> **What if the owner of the mobile home I'm renting fails to make payments?** Unfortunately, the mobile home can be repossessed. If repossessed while still containing your personal property, contact the person who owned the mobile home to determine how to reclaim it. Georgia law sets requirements on when to dispose of personal property inside a mobile home.

**Repossession of Mobile Homes and Lots:**
- When a mobile home owner only rents the mobile home but not the land, the owner must file a personal property foreclosure to repossess. The owner may not use the eviction process.
- The owner of the land can use the eviction process to regain possession of the land.
- **Abandonment:** A mobile home may be considered abandoned after being unattended for at least **ninety (90) days** without anyone paying rent, after removal of personal belongings, after cancellation of insurance, or after cancellation of utilities.[^17] If determined to be derelict, the landowner may provide notice to last known addresses and then remove the mobile home if the responsible party(ies) do not request a hearing within 90 days.[^18]

[^17]: O.C.G.A. 44-7-12
[^18]: O.C.G.A. 44-7-13

## Eviction Q&A

*This section provides answers to common questions if you are facing the eviction process, but it is not intended to be legal advice for a specific situation.*

**Q. I've been served with an eviction (dispossessory). Do I have to answer?**

A. If you do not file an answer within the time period specified on the notice, your landlord automatically wins the case, and an eviction will go on your record and the landlord may receive a money judgment for back rent or fees. If the landlord requested a money judgment but you were not served in person (served by "tack and mail"), the landlord may not be able to get a money judgment unless you file an answer or show up in court. Seek legal help if you can.

---

**Q. What if I answer late? What if I missed the deadline because I was out of town or in the hospital?**

A. It is usually very hard to get an extension if you do not file an answer on time, even if you had an emergency. You may be able to request permission to file an answer late if you were unaware the notice was served. You can file a "motion to set aside the judgment." Try to get legal help if possible.

---

**Q. I am being evicted. Is there anything I can do?**

A. Yes, you may have multiple defenses. You must assert any and all defenses in your answer to the eviction. **FAILURE TO ASSERT DEFENSES MAY ALLOW THE LANDLORD TO OBTAIN A JUDGMENT AGAINST YOU FOR FAILURE TO RAISE A DEFENSE.**

Possible defenses to raise in your answer include:

- **Lack of Notice/No Demand for Possession:** The landlord must demand that you vacate before filing for eviction. If evicting for failure to pay rent, the landlord must give written notice at least three (3) days before filing.
- **Improper Service:** If the landlord did not properly serve the eviction notice.
- **Tenancy-at-Will:** If you never had a written lease or it expired and the landlord permitted you to remain, the landlord must give you 60 days' notice to terminate. (Note: does not apply if evicting for nonpayment of rent.)
- **Amounts Owed:** You can assert that you do not owe the amounts alleged.
- **Complete Tender:** You may avoid eviction by paying all rent alleged plus court costs within seven (7) days of being served, if the landlord refused.
- **Not My Landlord:** If the person filing for eviction has lost the property to foreclosure and no longer owns it.
- **Accused of doing something "wrong" and lease terminated:** Challenge the violation factually or show that the incident did not violate the lease.
- **Living in public housing or receiving rental subsidies:** You may have the right to an informal hearing before the landlord can pursue eviction in the courts.
- **You paid rent but landlord claims you didn't:** Assert that you paid according to the lease or landlord's instructions.
- **Partial Payment:** The landlord cannot evict you for failure to pay if they accepted partial payment for the current month.
- **Waiver:** If you normally pay rent late and the landlord accepts it, assert a waiver defense.
- **Constructive Eviction:** If you vacated because the unit was uninhabitable and stopped paying rent.
- **Discrimination:** If you feel you are being discriminated against on the basis of a protected class.
- **Domestic violence victim status:** If you live in public housing or receive rental subsidies and are being evicted based on your status as a victim of domestic violence, dating violence, sexual assault, or stalking, assert protection under the Violence Against Women Act.

> **PERSONAL LIFE EVENTS ARE NOT A DEFENSE:** It is never a defense to eviction that you did not have the money, lost your job, or had other financial hardship. The judge may sympathize, but cannot stop the eviction if you did not pay your rent due to lack of funds.

---

**Q. I see that the answer form has a space for counterclaims. What are these?**

A. Counterclaims should be asserted if you feel that the landlord did something wrong and owes you damages or another remedy. Common counterclaims include:

- **Failure to repair:** If the landlord failed to repair a problem you told them about, calculate damages based on the unit's reduced value. You must prove you gave notice and a reasonable amount of time to fix it.
- **Damage to personal property:** If the landlord owes you money for damaged personal property due to flooding or other conditions.
- **Landlord Breach:** If your quiet enjoyment and use of the unit has decreased due to the landlord's actions (e.g., constant disruption, noisy neighbors, inability to use the unit as intended).
- **Landlord Retaliation:** If the landlord retaliated against you for requesting a repair or filing a complaint with code enforcement, you may claim one month's rent plus $500.

---

**Q. I'm going to court. How does this work?**

- **Plan ahead and arrive early.** Account for parking, security, and finding the correct courtroom.
- **Calendar call.** When your name is called, verbally respond and stand up. You, the tenant, will be the defendant. The landlord will be the plaintiff. Calendar call is not the time to argue your case.
- **Mediation.** Prior to the hearing, the court may require or suggest mediation. A mediator is not a judge and will only work with both parties to see if an agreement can be reached.
- **Presentation tips.** Keep your argument brief and concise. Be respectful. Focus on legal defenses, not sympathy stories. Do not argue with the judge.
- **Evidence.** Bring a copy of your lease, photographs, copies of messages to/from your landlord, receipts, and other documentation. Note that some judges do not allow cell phones in the courtroom, so you may need to print copies.
- **Witnesses (subpoena).** If a witness will not come willingly, you can have the court serve a "subpoena." Serve the subpoena at least 24 hours before the hearing. You may have to pay subpoenaed witnesses $25 a day plus 45 cents per mile if they live outside the county.
- **Hearsay.** The court generally will not allow testimony about what a third person said. If your roommate knows something relevant, they should attend and testify.
- **Judge's Order.** Get and keep a copy of the judge's order and follow what it says.
- **Appeals.** File within **seven (7) days** of the court's judgment. If you cannot pay court fees, file a "pauper's affidavit." To continue living in your unit while waiting for the appeal, you must pay all rent the court ordered. After filing the appeal, your case goes to state or superior court.

---

**Q. What is a move-out agreement?**

A. You and your landlord can make a move-out agreement either informally or as part of mediation. A move-out agreement settles the case and may become part of the court order. Typically, it reduces the amount you owe if you agree to move out by a certain date. If you do not comply with the terms of a court-ordered agreement, the landlord can immediately seek a "writ of possession" without another court hearing.

Make sure any counterclaims you have are addressed in the agreement, or they will be presumed dismissed.
